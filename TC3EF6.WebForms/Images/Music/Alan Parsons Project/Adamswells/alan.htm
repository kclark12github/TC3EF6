Alan Parsons first know work was with the Beatles on their "Abbey Road" album. Shortly thereafter, he began creating his own platinum selling albums with his group, "The Alan Parsons Project". Ever since I heard the Alan Parsons Project sing the song "Time" from their "Turn of a Friendly Card" album in 1980, I was hooked. After producing and engineering many CD's, each one is unique, and most have a theme to them. Alan has always given the customer a great album cover, so I decided to include a picture of the album cover, a short description of the theme of the album, along with my favorite (and SuperFave) songs from the album. Note: All opinions on themes are my own, except for Pyramid, which was taken from the inside tray card.

"Tales of Mystery and Imagination". Released in 1976, this was the first Alan Parsons Project album. It is fitting that my most favorite Alan Parsons song, "To One In Paradise", is on this album. Another favorite song on this album is "Doctor Tarr And Professor Fether". The theme for this album was dedicated to the works of Edgar Allan Poe. Alan decided to use the "concept album" approach after working with Pink Floyd on "The Dark Side Of The Moon".
"I Robot". 1977. The theme for this album is the rise of the machine and the decline of man. When man tried to create robot in his own image, he was imitating what God did many years before. This imitation theme is evident with the song entitled "Genesis Chapter 1 Verse 32". Genesis chapter one (also known to me as the "creation" chapter in the Holy Bible) only has 31 verses. Man tried to imitate Gods' creation with something artificial (a verse 32) and failed. "Some Other Time" is a favorite from this album.
"Pyramid", 1978. This album seeks to amplify the echoes of the past and explore the unsolved mysteries of the present. Pyramid...the last remaining wonder of the ancient world. Favorite songs include "What Goes Up", "The Eagle will Rise Again", "Can't Take It With You", and "Pyromania".
"Eve", 1979. You can guess the theme for this album (Hint: Women). Can't live with them, can't live without them. This album, "Try Anything Once", and "Freudiana" were the only albums which include songs where a woman sings the lead vocals. SuperFave song "Winding Me Up" and favorite song "If I Could Change Your Mind" are included on this album.
"Turn Of A Friendly Card", 1980. Life can sometimes be related to a game...a game where time and chance are as important as luck and skill. Favorites include "Time", the instrumental work "The Gold Bug", and the "Turn Of A Friendly Card" medley, including "The Ace Of Swords".
"Eye In The Sky", 1982. Think Big Brother is watching over you? Wondering what people are saying about you in other circles? Paranoia setting in from hearing whispers in the night? According to the general public, this was the best selling Alan Parsons Project album made. "Eye In The Sky", "Silence And I" and "Psychobabble" are my favorites on this album.
"Ammonia Avenue", 1983. Global warming, careless pollution, and lack of concern for one another must have prompted this album. This is my favorite Alan Parsons album. Favorite songs include "Prime Time", "Let Me Go Home", "One Good Reason", "Since The Last Goodbye", "Don't Answer Me", "Pipeline" and Superfave song "Dancing On A Highwire".
"Vulture Culture", 1984. In this dog-eat-dog world, it's every man for himself! Selfish song titles like "Separate Lives", "Vulture Culture" and "Let's Talk About Me" personify the greed and self-centered nature of mankind. Favorites include "Sooner or Later" and the instrumental work "Hawkeye" (especially that saxophone!)
"Keats", 1984. Alan Parsons only produced this album. I thought I would mention it here because Keats (the name of the group -and- the album) was mostly made up of musicians that had been in the Alan Parsons Project at one time or another. Keats took it's name from a West London restaurant that Alan frequented after recording sessions in the studio. Smooth sounding "Walking On Ice" is a favorite from this album.
"Stereotomy", 1985. In my understanding, this album could relate to someone with a dual personality, sometimes light, sometimes dark. Exploring the how and why of the swings between the two personalities are what this album expands on. Favorites include "Beaujolais", "Limelight", and "Light Of The World".
"Gaudi", 1987. This album was inspired by the life and works of Antonio Gaudi (1852-1926), whose Sagrada Familia Cathedral in Barcelona, Spain, involves a construction timetable which will run for hundreds of years. "La Sagrada Familia", "Standing On Higher Ground", and "Money Talks" (how true) are favorites from this album.
"Freudiana", 1990. Songwriter Eric Woolfson and Alan collaborated on a musical about the life of Sigmund Freud. The music inspired a musical/stage production that opened in Vienna in December, 1990. After this album, Eric left the Project to concentrate on musicals (Gaudi was made into one). Favorite songs include "Freudiana", "Little Hans", "Destiny", and Superfave "Funny You Should Say That".
"Try Anything Once", 1993. The Project is no more. Long time executive producer and songwriter Eric Woolfson has departed, prompting Alan to change the name of the group to simply "Alan Parsons". This doesn't mean the quality of music has diminished. After a few years off, a different style emerges in favorites "Breakaway", "Mr. Time", "Jigue", "Siren Song", "Back Against The Wall", and SuperFave song "Dreamscape".
"Live", 1995. Yes, it is a live album...but with a twist. Twelve songs are live versions of studio hits, but as a bonus, three new studio tracks are included. The live songs are worthy counterparts to their respective studio tracks, but I enjoy the three new tracks at the end the most. "When", "Take The Money And Run" (not the Steve Miller Band song), and "You're The Voice" make up this power packed trio of new tracks.
"On Air", 1996. The theme for this album was actually born out of an accident. Guitar player Ian Bairnson's cousin was flying his helicopter on a peace mission in Iraq and was shot down by friendly fire. Ian wrote the song "Brother Up In Heaven", and Alan decided the album theme would be about flight. Included in the United States version of the album was a multimedia CD-ROM that included video snippets with background music from the songs from the album. Favorites include "Can't Look Down", "Too Close to the Sun", "Fall Free", and "Blue Blue Sky".

 

Three more compilation albums were released by the Alan Parsons Project. "The Best Of The Alan Parsons Project", 1983. "The Best Of The Alan Parsons Project, Volume Two", 1987. "The Instrumental Works", 1988.
