The Grumman F-14 Tomcat is the world's premier carrier-based fighter/interceptor. Its missions include Combat Air Patrol, Maritime Air Superiority, Deck-Launched Intercept, Tactical Reconnaissance, and recently its latent ground-attack capability has finally been tapped by the US Navy.

The F-14 is a two-seat, twin engine, supersonic aircraft with variable-geometry wings. The two crew members are seated in tandem inside the cockpit covered by a large one-piece canopy. The canopy gives excellent visibility all around with negligible distortion. On the starboard side of the fuselage just ahead of the cockpit is the retractable in-flight refuelling probe. On the port side the boarding ladder and steps are located below the cockpit. Several small sensory tubes are mounted on both sides of the forward fuselage.

The engines are deliberately set wide apart and each is contained within its own nacelle; thus reduces the detrimental effect a damaged engine would have on the other one. Although the trough between the two engine nacelles imposes drag penalty, it also provides additional lift, conformal weapons attachment, and extra storage space for fuel to the aircraft. The rear fuselage area sandwiched between the engine nacelles is known as the pancake. The pancake provides some 40% additional lift to the airframe. The rear end of the pancake is the beaver tail, which contains a fuel-dump pipe and a small ECM (Electronic Counter Measure) antenna fairing. Located both above and below the pancake are the speed brakes. The lower speed brake is split into two halves to accommodate the arrestor hook. Right next to the hook are the chaff/flare dispensers.

The two rectangular-shaped air intakes offer a direct airflow path to the engines. When viewed from the side, the intakes are sharply raked from top to bottom, ensuring that sufficient amount of air will get into the engines at high angles of attack. Viewed from straight ahead, the top portions of the intakes are tilted towards the centerline of the aircraft and the bottom parts are canted outwards. The adequate separation of the intakes reduces interference between intake airflow and the fuselage boundary layer.

The shoulder-mounted wings are controlled by computer for automatic sweep during flight; a manual override is provided to the pilot in case of emergency or other special circumstances. The normal sweep range is 20º to 68º, a 75º oversweep position is available for stowage aboard the carrier. The wings sport full-span leading-edge slats, plain trailing-edge flaps, lift spoilers and dumpers. The F-14 does not have conventional ailerons. The primary method of roll control is by differential deflection of the all-moving horizontal stabilizers, supplemented by the wing spoilers located along the trailing-edge of the upper wing surface. When the wing sweep is greater than 57º the horizontal tailplanes operate alone to provide roll, while the wing spoilers are used during lower speed and angles of wing sweep (less than 57º).

Two small retractable triangular-shaped vanes are mounted on the leading-edge of the wing gloves. These glove vanes are extended automatically at supersonic speed (between mach 1 and mach 1.4) to generate additional lift ahead of the aircraft's center of gravity and to push the nose up. By doing so they help to reduce the loads on the tailplanes, therefore maintaining the Tomcat's turning performance . However, these vanes were subsequently deleted from the B(A+) and D models to provide extra space for fuel and ECM equipments.

Inside each intake there are two variable-angle ramps and an air-bleed door located on the ceiling of the inlets. The ramps are controlled by an air inlet computer, which calculates the optimal ramp positions based upon the air temperature, air pressure, engine speed, and the angle of attack. Excess air is diverted out of the duct through the air-bleed door.

Underneath the nose of the F-14 A and B(A+), a TCS (Television Camera Set) pod is carried along with a small position light and ECM fairing. In the D model, a dual chin pod is used to house both the TCS and the new IRST (InfraRed Search and Track) system. The position light and ECM antenna were retained.

The Tomcat's armaments include the obligatory M61A1 20mm cannon. The cannon muzzle and assembly are located on the port side of the fuselage, just ahead of the boarding ladder. For short-range air-to-air engagements, up to four AIM-9 Sidewinders can be carried on the wing glove pylons. As many as six medium-range AIM-7 Sparrow missiles can be fitted in the four missile wells underneath the fuselage and slung below the two glove pylons. The F-14 is the only aircraft in the world capable of carrying the ultra long-range AIM-54 Phoenix air-to-air missiles. As with the Sparrows, the aircraft can carry up to two of them below the wing glove pylons, and four underneath the fuselage with special adaptor pallets.

In recent years the Navy had finally decided to utilize the Tomcat's latent ground-attack capability. Its air-to-ground arsenal include the Mk. 80 series iron bombs, cluster bomb units, and various laser-guided munitions. The recent addition of the LANTIRN (Low Altitude Navigation/Targeting InfraRed for Night) pod system has transformed the Tomcat into a precision strike aircraft.
