Notable Events In
Tomcat's Service Career


Prototype Number One's second and final flight.
An VF-32 Tomcat went swimming.

The first F-14 prototype aircraft (BuNo 157980) made its maiden flight on the late afternoon of 21 December 1970. Grumman chief test pilot Bob Smythe was the pilot, while project test pilot Bill Miller sat in the RIO's seat. The aircraft took to the air with wings fixed in the unswept (20º) position and carried four dummy Sidewinder missiles. Due to poor weather and imminent sunset the flight was relatively brief. The uneventful first flight could not have prepared Grumman for what would happen next.

Nine days later on 30 December, prototype Number One went aloft for its second flight--this time with Miller at the helm and Smythe in the back seat. It left the runway at 1018, the landing gear was retracted after the control and stability checks were performed. The Tomcat proceeded towards the assigned flight test area.

At around 1043, a chase plane noticed a trail of fluid was streaming from the aircraft. As the chase plane maneuvered to get a closer look, Miller reported a primary hydraulic system failure; he immediately aborted the mission and headed home.

When they were four miles from the Calverton runway, the pilots used the emergency nitrogen bottle to blow down the landing gear. Just then the unthinkable happened--the secondary hydraulic system also failed. Using the Combat Survival System--a last-resort control system driven by an electrical pump and used to control only the tailerons and rudders--Miller attempted to land the aircraft. But on the final approach, even this limited control system was showing signs of failing! It had become obvious to the crew that the aircraft was not going to make it and it was flying into a dense forest just short of the runway. They had no choice but to punch out. Smythe ejected when the aircraft was only a mere 25 ft above the trees, while Miller got out less than half a second before impact. Fortunately they suffered only superficial injuries and both remained with the F-14 program.

The ensuing official investigations showed that fatigue failures of the pipes in both hydraulic systems which led to the partial failure of the flight controls was the culprit. But given the fact that the aircraft had suffered no combat damage, and the two hydraulic systems were deliberately designed to be set wide apart, the chances of failure in both systems were very remote. After further investigation, Mike Pelehach and his team finally discovered the problem: When the engine was set to idle mode during test flights, its idle RPM happened to be on the same frequency as the nine-cylinder pump which worked off the engine. That exact frequency happened to be the one that would cause the lines to naturally resonate, the pipes vibrated, developed metal fatigue, then broke. Since the secondary hydraulic system was built as a mirror image of the primary, it too failed for the same reason.

From that day on, the redundant hydraulic lines in aircraft would be implemented differently--a lesson learned from that crash.

In September 1976, an VF-32 Tomcat (BuNo 159588) made headline news when its engines roared out of control while taxiing across the deck of USS Kennedy and the aircraft plunged into 1,850 ft of water, took along with it a priceless Phoenix missile. The crew ejected safely. At the time of the event, the Kennedy was operating in international waters and nearby Soviet naval ships had witnessed the entire incident.

Fearing the Tomcat and its Phoenix missile may fall into Soviet hands, the Navy immediately began its recovery operation. A ten-day sonar search by the first wave of salvage vessels located the Tomcat, but adverse weather suspended the recovery effort. When the operation resumed, the aircraft was relocated but at a different location. Under the watchful eye of the Soviet ships, US Navy's nuclear-powered NR-1 research submarine went down to inspect the aircraft. It hooked a cable onto the Tomcat's undercarriage, then a British vessel attempted to pull the aircraft to the surface. The cable snapped, so a stronger line was used on a second attempt, but the cable broke again! Clearly a better equipped salvage ship was needed.

While waiting for the proper recovery vessel to arrive, the NR-1 retrieved the Phoenix missile which had become separated from the aircraft. When the two West German ships--Taurus and Twyford--arrived on the scene, the operation resumed. They made firm attachment to the wreckage, but this time instead of trying to winch it to the surface at once, they lifted it half way up then slowly dragged the aircraft to shallow waters, from where it was carefully hauled to the surface. The entire operation lasted almost two months and cost two and a half million dollars.

While one reason for such an expensive and time-consuming endeavor was the need to examine the wreckage and determine the cause of the accident, it is unquestionably true that the overriding concern was to prevent some of America's most sophisticated weaponry from being compromised.
